## 2. Competitor Profiles

### Eventbrite
Acquired by Bending Spoons (Italian software roll-up) for $500M, closed March 10, 2026 ([Skift](https://meetings.skift.com/2025/12/02/bending-spoons-to-acquire-eventbrite-in-500-million-cash-deal/)). Significant US headcount cut; product is mid-overhaul: faster creation, redesigned creator profiles with cover images and trust badges, AI description/image/ad copy generation (claimed 30% faster campaign launch), multi-slot scanning (April 2026), offline tickets, and mobile wallet integration on the roadmap. Fees: 3.7% + $1.79/ticket + 2.9% payment processing per order (effective ~8–10% for a €50 ticket) — shown to buyer by default ([SimpleTix](https://www.simpletix.com/eventbrite-fees-explained-2026/)). Instant Payouts exist (opt-in, 3% fee, US-first) but standard payouts are held until after the event — the single largest organizer complaint ([Comus.io](https://comus.io/event-management/eventbrite-reviews-summary-fees-payouts/)). No per-org subdomain; no white-label. Operates a consumer discovery marketplace giving organizers organic reach — this is its biggest structural advantage over all white-label-only alternatives. Under Bending Spoons, expect continued AI acceleration and cost-optimization (post-acquisition cost culture), but also continued platform-reliability and support-quality deterioration per current reviews ([Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.eventbrite.com)).

### DICE FM
Music/culture-first mobile-only platform. Acquired by Fever (global discovery, 300M users, 40 countries) in June 2025 for undisclosed sum ([Newsroom Fever](https://newsroom.feverup.com/en-US/250537-fever-and-dice-join-forces-to-build-a-live-entertainment-tech-powerhouse/)). Fee: 10–15% embedded into displayed price (no checkout sticker shock; claimed +30% checkout conversion). Anti-scalping via Locked Ticket (dynamic QR activates 2hrs before event, no off-app transfer). Waitlist is the resale mechanism: controlled face-value return through the queue. DICE Extras (2025): ancillary upsell marketplace (parking, merch, VIP, pre-ordered drinks) growing ~25% YoY ([Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/dice-launches-dice-extras-tool-as-part-of-new-dice-in-app-marketplace/)). Discovery moat is strongest value prop for organizers (Spotify/Apple Music integration + Fever network). Not self-serve: invite/curated. Post-Fever acquisition, indie organizers report the platform feeling more commercial ([RA](https://ra.co/news/82812)). App-only tickets frustrate buyers who want PDF QR fallback.

### Shotgun
Paris-founded (2014), Europe's dominant nightlife/electronic platform. €200M+ processed annually across 49 countries, 5.5M registered users, 15M+ tickets sold in 2024 ([Stripe case study](https://stripe.com/customers/shotgun)). Uses Stripe Connect as the marketplace backbone: organizers have direct access to revenue from first sale, vendor/artist disbursements, Stripe Terminal/Tap to Pay for on-site F&B (2025). Smartboard analytics (real-time sales, demographic segmentation, dynamic audience segments, tracking links per channel). Dynamic segments can gate ticket tiers to defined audiences — member-only releases, early-bird for past attendees. Promoters Management module: named sub-promoter portals with stats. Integrated resale: waiting list, Resale Upgrade (above-face resale where Shotgun takes 30% of the markup at [support-pro.shotgun.live](https://support-pro.shotgun.live/hc/en-us/articles/14778009789458-The-Resale-Upgrade)). Membership programs (recurring pass-as-event, gated tiers). Primary organizer fee: not publicly stated, likely negotiated. No NFT/blockchain. No per-org subdomain (embeddable widget exists but hosted pages are on shotgun.live).

### Fatsoma
Manchester-founded (2005), UK-dominant, 40,000+ organizers, 3M+ app downloads. Rooted in student nightlife. Fee: 10% + minimum £1 per ticket (organizer can absorb or pass to buyer) ([ticketing.fatsoma.com](https://ticketing.fatsoma.com/pricing)). Professional tier from £1,500/year adds branded website + iOS/Android apps. Rep system is the standout feature: unlimited reps, organizer-set commission per ticket type, rep-only tiers, auto-payout 3 days post-event, per-rep click/revenue/commission analytics ([ticketing.fatsoma.com/f/reps](https://ticketing.fatsoma.com/f/reps)). Follower auto-acquisition: every ticket purchase auto-follows the organizer; Fatsoma Chat enables group pre-event coordination + organizer-to-buyer messaging. Event Announcements: simultaneous push + email to all followers (claimed ~50% of ticket sales for established brands). NFT Collectible add-on: organizers attach purchasable souvenir NFTs at checkout — pure collectible/upsell, no smart-contract access control or royalties ([ticketing.fatsoma.com/f/nfts](https://ticketing.fatsoma.com/f/nfts)). No native first-party resale; routes buyers to Tixel for secondary sales — a significant gap vs. Shotgun.

### Tito (ti.to)
Developer-conference-focused (SmashingConf, GitNation, Mozilla, Shopify). Single flat 3% fee, no monthly subscription, no feature gating, nonprofit rate 2.5%. Fee capped at €25/ticket above ~€833 ([ti.to/pricing](https://ti.to/pricing)). Organizer connects own Stripe account via Connect; Tito never holds funds — rolling 2–7 day Stripe payout. Strongest embeddable widget in this competitive set (Web Components, lifecycle callbacks, field prefill, ticket filtering, unstyled CSS mode). Full Admin + Check-in API, real-time webhooks, Salesforce integration. No secondary market, no NFT, no subdomain storefront, no consumer discovery. The model that makes Tito credible is identical to Miso's planned direction: Stripe Connect with organizer owning their Stripe identity, platform fee billed separately.

### Billetweb
French-origin (since 2005), strong penetration in French associations, schools, cultural venues, student organizations. Fee: €0.29 + 1% per ticket (€0.79 on a €50 ticket — cheapest in European market at comparable feature depth). In-person/counter sales: free. Manual order entry: free. Payments via Lemonway (French ACPR-regulated); payouts twice monthly (1st and 16th) — slower than Stripe's rolling payout ([billetweb.fr/tarifs](https://www.billetweb.fr/fr-fr/tarifs/)). Includes at base rate: CRM up to 100K contacts, audience segmentation, numbered seating (€0.10/ticket), multi-event shopping cart, cashless on-site payments (€1/participant), Google Analytics + Facebook Pixel, refunds, email to buyers. French data residency: servers in France/EU — decisive for public bodies and loi 1901 associations. No secondary market, no NFT, no subdomain storefront. Competitive threat to Miso only on pure price for French market; loses on every differentiation dimension.

### Tixel
Australian-founded (2018), US expansion launched June 2025 ([IQ Magazine](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/06/ticket-resale-platform-tixel-in-us-expansion/)). Fan-to-fan resale with organizer integration: events can embed Tixel resale directly. Fees: seller 5.9% (min $4.50 US), buyer 6.9%. Price cap: 10% above face value in Australia; US jurisdiction-dependent. Instant payout option for sellers (small fee). 14,000+ unique events in 2024. No primary ticketing; pure secondary/resale layer. Positioned as the integration-friendly resale partner for organizers on any primary platform — the "official resale" button. Fatsoma routes its resale to Tixel ([support.fatsoma.com](https://support.fatsoma.com/support/solutions/articles/60000761074-can-i-sell-my-tickets-to-someone-else-)). Relevant to Miso as a potential integration/acquisition threat if Miso's per-org resale marketplace is slow to launch.

### TicketSwap
Dominant European fan-to-fan exchange. Buyer: 7% service + 3% transaction = 10% total. Seller: 5%. Default price cap: 120% of face value (20% above); face-value-only enforced in Belgium, France, Ireland, Poland, Denmark ([help.ticketswap.com](https://help.ticketswap.com/en/articles/5123890-pricing-rules-a-quick-overview)). FairShare is its strongest organizer feature: 80/20 or 50/50 above-face profit split back to organizer — the non-blockchain equivalent of Miso's royalty model ([IQ Magazine](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2023/04/ticketswap-fairshare-profit-split-promoter/)). Organizer integrations let orgs set price floors, enable "Shared Resale" pools, and embed TicketSwap as official resale channel. Paradiso (Amsterdam) and Le Guess Who? are named venue partners. The FairShare model won a Dutch Superstar Award 2023. TicketSwap is the main competitive benchmark for Miso's resale royalty mechanic — but it operates as an external resale partner, not a primary ticketing platform with integrated resale.

### On-Chain Ticketing Survivors

**OPEN Ticketing Ecosystem (formerly GET Protocol):** Issued 5M+ tickets on-chain, 200+ events/month, 121 countries. CM.com acquired GUTS Tickets (the consumer brand) in February 2025; OPEN retained the protocol/API layer and now operates as pure B2B infrastructure ([CM.com press release](https://www.cm.com/press/cmcom-acquires-guts-tickets/)). Supports Polygon, Base, Solana, Tezos, Sophon. Integrates with Crossmint (email-to-custodial-wallet) for invisible NFT onboarding. The canonical invisible-NFT model at production scale: rotating QR codes (screenshot-proof), buyer sees no blockchain branding, organizer gets resale royalties. Token ($OPN) is burned per ticket issued — economic alignment with protocol growth.

**SeatlabNFT:** NEAR Protocol. Issued 150,000 NFT tickets for a UK music festival (March 2025) via NFC wristbands. White-label for organizers: listing, minting, primary sales free (NEAR sub-$0.01 gas). Organizer resale royalties. $SEAT governance token. Maintains separate non-blockchain seat reservation product (Seatlab) for buyers who don't want NFT layer.

**YellowHeart:** Raised $9.2M. Processed tickets for Maroon 5, Kings of Leon, MGM Resorts, Blue Note Jazz Club, Burnley FC. Core pitch: resale royalties flow back to artists/organizers automatically on secondary trades. Operating in 2025–2026.

## 3. Strategic Reads

### Where Miso Is Genuinely Differentiated

**1. Org-first subdomain storefront as the default unit.** No mainstream competitor (Eventbrite, DICE, Shotgun, Fatsoma, Tito, Billetweb) gives every organizer a self-serve branded subdomain as the baseline product — not as a paid add-on, not gated behind £1,500/year (Fatsoma Professional), not a plus-tier upsell (Luma). Ticket Tailor is the closest peer; Vivenu/Eventcube serve the enterprise tier. This framing — "your own box office, not a listing on our marketplace" — is a defensible positioning statement that addresses the top organizer complaint about Eventbrite (no control, always Eventbrite-branded, no identity).

**2. Stripe Connect separate charges + transfers with explicit platform fee.** Eventbrite is a payment facilitator — organizers don't hold a Stripe account; funds are pooled and released after the event. DICE uses proprietary rails. Fatsoma: payout details vague. Billetweb: Lemonway pooled escrow with bi-monthly payouts. Only Tito (organizer's own Stripe, Tito bills separately) and Shotgun (Stripe Connect, direct access from first sale) are in the same category. When Miso's separate charges + transfers migration is complete, it eliminates the #1 Eventbrite complaint structurally — not as an opt-in 3% extra fee but as the default operating model. This is worth communicating explicitly to prospective organizers ([Eventbrite Instant Payouts context](https://investor.eventbrite.com/press-releases/press-releases-details/2024/Eventbrite-Introduces-Instant-Payouts-and-Tap-to-Pay-a-Transformative-Duo-That-Delivers-a-Win-Win-Experience-for-Event-Organizers-and-Attendees/default.aspx)).

**3. Per-org resale marketplace with organizer-set royalties.** No mainstream competitor has this configuration. TicketSwap FairShare achieves the economic outcome (organizer gets 80% of above-face markup) but operates as an external partner integration. OPEN Protocol achieves programmable smart-contract royalties but as B2B infrastructure sold to other ticketing companies. Miso combines primary ticketing + captive resale marketplace + organizer-controlled royalty rate in one platform — and the ERC-2981 royalty enforcement is more reliable within a captive marketplace than on open NFT markets where royalties are often bypassed ([sciencedirect.com, HBR 2025](https://hbr.org/2025/12/a-new-era-for-the-digital-resale-market)). The royalty rate (5–10% is the smart-contract norm; TicketSwap's FairShare is 80/20 above-face) represents a direct new revenue line for organizers that no competitor at this tier offers.

**4. ERC-721 tickets on Base as invisible infrastructure.** Fatsoma's NFT collectible is a bolt-on upsell (no access control, no royalties). DICE/Eventbrite/Shotgun/Tito/Billetweb have zero on-chain layer. The production best-practice model — buyer sees "digital ticket," never touches a wallet, rotating QR validates against chain state — is what GUTS/OPEN built at 5M+ tickets and is the architecture Miso follows. Base Sepolia's sub-$0.01 gas and sub-200ms block times make this viable at scale ([dextools guide](https://www.dextools.io/tutorials/what-is-base-chain-coinbase-l2-guide-2026)). Future composability (loyalty airdrops, token-gated presales, DeFi event financing) is optionality that competitors cannot retrofit.

**5. Human support at current scale.** Eventbrite's support is now predominantly AI bots post-Bending Spoons headcount cuts; phone support effectively gone. DICE: no phone, slow email post-Fever acquisition. Shotgun/Fatsoma: mixed reviews. Personal onboarding for a smaller organizer base is a genuine differentiator that costs nothing extra and compounds with every organizer who publicly talks about it. This advantage decays as Miso scales — document and operationalize it now.

### Table-Stakes Gaps Most Damaging to Organizer Acquisition

**1. No follower/subscriber mechanic.** Fatsoma auto-follows every ticket buyer; simultaneous push + email announcements claimed to drive ~50% of ticket sales for established Fatsoma brands. Shotgun has newsletters + push via Smartboard. Without a follow/subscriber layer, every new event for a Miso organizer starts from zero — the organizer must drive traffic themselves. This is the single largest friction point for retaining organizers who have run their first event successfully and want to grow.

**2. No integrated waitlist for sold-out events.** DICE's waitlist is the gold standard; Shotgun has a waiting list with auto-charge; TicketSwap's shared resale pool fills the same function. A sold-out Miso event has no mechanism to capture excess demand or convert it into goodwill. This is also a missed resale royalty trigger — the waitlist is the natural entry point to Miso's per-org resale marketplace.

**3. No promo codes / early bird releases.** Basic organizer expectation present on every competitor including the cheapest (Billetweb includes it free). This is a hard blocker for professional organizers using phased release strategies.

**4. No embeddable checkout widget.** Organizers with existing websites (WordPress, Squarespace, Webflow) expect to embed checkout without redirecting buyers off-site. Tito's widget is the technical benchmark. Absence pushes organizers toward Tito for tech-first audiences and Ticket Tailor for everyone else.

**5. No email marketing / event announcement system.** Eventbrite (Pro), Billetweb (free CRM), Fatsoma (push + email), Shotgun (Smartboard newsletters) all provide this. For organizers who rely on repeat audiences, this is a prerequisite.

### Regulatory Watch Items

**UK Ticket Tout Ban Bill (most immediate):** King's Speech (May 2026) included a draft Bill for consultation, not primary legislation. Full law unlikely before 2027–28 parliamentary session ([InsiderSport](https://insidersport.com/2026/05/14/uk-ticket-touting-kings-speech/)). When enacted: resale at above face value prohibited, resale platform service fees capped, quantity purchase limits. Miso's organizer-royalty resale model is compatible with a face-value cap regime — royalty is funded by the platform fee on a face-value transaction, not by above-face markup. This is a structural advantage over Shotgun's Resale Upgrade (30% of above-face markup) and Viagogo-style open markets. **Action:** Do not build resale mechanics that depend on above-face markup revenue as the margin model.

**EU Digital Fairness Act (Q4 2026 expected proposal):** 130+ industry figures signed an open letter to EU Justice Commissioner McGrath (January 2026) demanding the Act explicitly address industrial-scale scalping ([Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/pro/eu-ticket-resale-open-letter-2026-digital-fairness-act/)). Belgium's resale ban is under CJEU review (December 2025 referral); ruling will determine whether national bans are compatible with EU free movement of services — with implications for every platform operating in Belgium, France, Ireland, Spain, Poland, Denmark. **Action:** Per-country price cap configuration must be in the resale architecture from day one. TicketSwap's country-level enforcement (100% face value in Belgium/France etc., 120% elsewhere) is the reference implementation.

**FTC Junk Fees Rule (US, effective May 2025):** All-in pricing mandatory on initial listing pages; per-instance fines up to $53,088 ([FTC rule](https://vivenu.com/blog/2026-ticketing-laws-compliance-guide)). Even if Miso is not currently US-facing, this sets the global disclosure norm DICE already follows. **Action:** Verify checkout shows total fees (platform fee + Stripe processing) before the payment step, not just on the confirmation page.

**France national resale cap:** Resale at more than 10% above face value permitted only on authorized platforms. Miso's EU operations and French-market organizers (relevant given Billetweb's French stronghold) need this configuration in the resale marketplace.

**EU dynamic pricing scrutiny:** Ticketmaster's Platinum Tickets under EU regulatory review for transparency. Any AI price suggestion feature must show historical price range and explicit pricing model disclosure to avoid regulatory exposure.

## 4. Ranked Top-10 Candidate Features for Miso

**1. Integrated waitlist for sold-out events**  
Rationale: Unlocks the most direct path into Miso's resale marketplace; DICE proved it drives fan loyalty and repeat usage; sold-out events are the highest-leverage moment for organizer retention.

**2. Organizer follower / subscriber mechanic (auto-follow on purchase + event announcement push/email)**  
Rationale: Without it, every event is a cold start; Fatsoma's auto-follow model demonstrably drives ~50% of repeat-event ticket sales; most damaging table-stakes gap for organizer lifetime value.

**3. Promo codes + phased / early-bird ticket release scheduling**  
Rationale: Hard blocker for professional organizers; present on every competitor including the cheapest; absence alone loses deals to Tito and Billetweb.

**4. Embeddable checkout widget (JS embed, organizer's own website)**  
Rationale: Prerequisite for tech-adjacent organizers (developers, agencies); Tito's Web Components widget is the benchmark; absence cedes the "embed on my site" use case entirely to Tito and Ticket Tailor.

**5. 3–5 curated visual theme presets + color/font pickers for billeterie**  
Rationale: Ticket Tailor shipped this April 2025; Luma has 40+; for non-designers this is the "feels branded" shortcut that makes the subdomain storefront feel premium vs. a default template.

**6. Dynamic / rotating QR codes (screenshot-proof ticket validation)**  
Rationale: GUTS/OPEN's production-proven model at 5M+ tickets; ERC-721 ownership provides provenance but a static QR is still screenshot-vulnerable; rotation is a separate implementation layer on top of the NFT architecture.

**7. Per-country resale price cap configuration (face-value enforcement for EU jurisdictions)**  
Rationale: Belgium (CJEU pending), France (10% cap), Spain (profit ban), UK (draft bill) — Miso's EUR market is directly exposed; TicketSwap's country-level enforcement is the reference; must be in architecture before resale marketplace scales.

**8. AI event description + marketing copy generation (in-product, native flow)**  
Rationale: Table stakes in 2026 per B2B SaaS analysts; Eventbrite has it live; low implementation cost via LLM API; native in-product flow has more adoption than "copy from ChatGPT"; directly reduces organizer time-to-publish.

**9. Custom domain CNAME mapping (tickets.organizer.com → billeterie)**  
Rationale: Luma gates this behind $59/month; Ticket Tailor charges ~$10–15/month extra; offering it included at Miso's ~5% fee would be a strong differentiator for professional organizers and reinforces the "your own box office" positioning.

**10. Post-event digital collectible / attendance badge airdrop (ERC-721, buyer-invisible)**  
Rationale: Leverages existing NFT infrastructure at near-zero marginal cost on Base; Ticketmaster minted 5M+ collectibles; Fatsoma sells NFT collectibles as a paid upsell; gives organizers a loyalty and CRM tool that competitors charge extra for or don't offer at all; low regulatory risk vs. resale mechanics.
